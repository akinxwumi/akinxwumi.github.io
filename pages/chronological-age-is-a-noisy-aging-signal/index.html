Chronological age is a noisy aging signal

The traditional way of tracking age and, by extension, aging is to do a ++1 operation for each complete Gregorian year from the moment of birth. Socially, we even go out of our way to commemorate this, as if it were a good indicator of growing older or that the brutal reality of it is something worth celebrating [1]. No, it isn’t, and we should tweak this norm at scale. I think a good first step will be to recalibrate why and how we track age so that we can instead focus more purposefully on longevity [2].

They say you get what you measure. We should track our ages with the main intention of monitoring and extending our lifespan [3], and in order to do this effectively, we must rely on biological instead of chronological markers. The reason to use epigenetic clocks instead of atomic clocks is simply that tracking the body’s genetic activities is more reliable in the context of aging [4]. Ironically, focusing on numerical age is like tracking time with the sun, which we don’t anymore because we’ve found ways of keeping time that are more precise [5].

There is also another benefit low-key, apart from the fact that biological age is more qualitative. Once there is a shift in our mindset, we’ll have more control over the aging process, and it will become less and less of a fait accompli. We will be able to actionably reduce our aging rate, so living will progressively become less fatal. As of right now, because we tend to evaluate age temporally, we optimize for time, which is not under our control to a large extent. It is a heavenly constraint that we haven’t figured out how to overcome yet. On the other hand, thankfully, human biochemistry is more malleable. For instance, it has been shown that we can actively stimulate our brains’ neuroplasticity, which can help maintain and even improve our cognitive abilities as we continue to grow older [6].

This, now, is what I am attempting to do on a personal level across the board, even though my efforts are still objectively crude by frontier standards. Nonetheless, when next you ask me about my age, don’t be surprised if I struggle to answer you accurately.

Notes

[1] It is even more absurd now that I think of it. Going by the logic of this ritual, the more reasonable thing would be to start counting an individual’s age from the moment of conception. Of course, the constraint is that you don’t immediately know when this happens, but you can add a constant 9 months retroactively.

Raji noted that celebrating being closer to death and thus escaping this reality might be a good thing if you have a fatalistic attitude, which is a fair point.

[2] By “longevity,” I am referring to the slowing of aging at the very least and reversing it ultimately. There are already incredible people pushing this frontier in their own different ways. A few examples are David Sinclair, Bryan Johnson, Laura Deming, and Peter Attia.

I think eventually we must unbundle age. We should have standardized metrics for directly tracking the biological, psychological, and chronological progress of humans, among many other factors. I suspect that there are concrete works that address this already or are at least underway that I am unaware of.

[3] Personally, my health span is even more significant than my life span. I don’t particularly care about the length of time I live as long as good health is uniformly distributed across it.

[4] I am not suggesting that both chronological and biological ages are mutually exclusive. Rather, chronological age has a lower signal-to-noise ratio than biological (epigenetic) age. Biological age more accurately shows if you are at lower or higher risk for something compared to a healthy person of the same chronological age.

While the age number gives a general sense of aging and life’s progress, a strict focus on it obscures far more than it reveals about the qualitative nature, experiences, and maturation happening within a being.

[5] It is hard to imagine people telling time by observing the position of the sun relative to Earth nowadays. Or, better yet, estimate the location at sea with dead reckoning rather than GPS. This is because even though these methods can be useful, they can also introduce needless mistakes and inaccuracies that can have fatal consequences. As an example, the longitude act, which played a large part in resolving the longitude problem, was passed because of a shipwreck that occurred in 1707. Approx 2,000 lives were lost, but this is nothing compared to those that we lose currently due to our failure to more accurately gauge aging.

[6] The benefit of reducing our aging rate cannot be overemphasized. Aging is non-linear. To quote Bryan Johnson, “Even just being slightly above an aging rate of 1 biological year can increase your risk of death by 56% in the next 7 years and your risk of a chronic disease diagnosis by 54% over the next 7 years.”

There is good news: this recent academic study plausibly suggests that we are yet to reach the maximum limit to human lifespan, and there might not even be one.

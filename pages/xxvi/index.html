XXVI

Published on April 14, 2023.

Sometimes on your birthday, you can have the most profound awareness. For example on 7/11/2012 which was in my second year at the university, I came to the conclusion that schooling in Unilorin was not just terrible but also irredeemable and the prudent thing is to quit the system regardless of the sunk cost.

To get in and out of the university campus, for instance, you’d have to spend several hours on queue jostling for scarce taxis with most of your fellow students. If you were able to finally get in, you’d then have to contend for space (all the best with seats) in overcrowded lecture theaters (sometimes up to 3X capacity) only for lecturers to typically arrive late, give tepid, redundant lessons and then leave.

So on that fateful day, the STA 213 (Statistics for Engineering and Physical Science) lecturer abruptly changed the lecture venue 3 different times and we had to run helter-skelter over a significant distance for about 30 minutes in different directions. That was my breaking point as it became at that moment untenable for me to continue learning under such a pedagogic system!

Today, eight years later, I had come to another realization: living and doing business for me in Nigeria is not only extremely volatile (my cousin once said it is a war zone) but perhaps more poignantly, there is no corresponding upside.

https//miro.medium.com/max/1238/1d9YzcIWtXOFwJMoERBEt8A.jpeg 26 has always been the threshold for me

Two days earlier, while driving to Wisdom’s house, I accidentally turned into a one-way road at the behest of Google Maps and for the first time in my life, I was arrested, arraigned before a mobile court, and subsequently, one of our business cars was forfeited to the Lagos state government. On my way back home today after the entire ordeal, I learned that a petrol tanker had for the umpteenth time exploded earlier along the Lagos-Ibadan expressway. Several persons probably died and countless vehicles were burnt including those of a car dealership with about 10–15 cars. I just can’t help but think of how hostile the Nigerian environment is.

For example, the present economic policies (fiscal and monetary) have been devastating to our car financing business — Nairalife. The current inflation rate is over 25% and the naira was recently devalued by about 30% by the CBN while also turning a blind eye to the foreign-exchange “parallel market”. Asides from these, we’ve also had to deal with numerous defaults from our customers and if we factor in the total loss incurred on the just forfeited car (~ 2m nairas), we are probably going to lose about 40–60% of our invested capital to a combination of inflation, devaluation, depreciation of our vehicles, loan impairment and more.

The future is very gloomy and in recent times, I’ve often found myself just crying into the abyss. I even sometimes wonder if and how others are doing better under these conditions and then ask myself how I managed to fuck things up this much! It seems I am failing in all areas of my life and I occasionally feel cursed as a result — the higher the uncertainty, the more superstitious every human tends to become. I think I might even have an anxiety disorder.

https//miro.medium.com/max/720/1qAJkB4LYZS5Zfya7_nJ3zA.jpeg

Mr Shola — a business owner in my area put it best; “to survive in Nigeria, you have to be a stone.” I doubt if I’m made of stone.

I and Wisdom will have another go at it all again with Bookmakr (a tool we’ve been building on the side since after the lockdown) but alas, this is my last attempt as I don’t have more than one more fight in me!

Akin Wumi

The path of least resistance

I went to a friend’s place yesterday for Salah. He’s an old friend, and we have not hung out in a very long time since after university, so even though I’m naturally very reluctant to leave my house, I obliged him when he asked me to come over. It was an opportunity to catch up, to discuss the vicissitudes of life, what that has translated to in our individual lives, the opportunities that lie ahead of us, and most importantly, to pass this solemn, although implicit, pledge that we are together in this struggle to survive.

This friend of mine has a very young and precocious brother that got admitted to the JSS 1 class of a federal government college in the axis of Ilorin while we were in the university, and since my friend schools in Ilorin, their dad made sure he was responsible for his brother’s upkeep. He sends money to my friend, and he in turn takes him to and from his school after each break and gets him school stuff—books, clothes, food, and all. You know the drill. So sometimes his brother had to stay with us, and I had some interactions with him during those years.

This was some 6–7 years ago if I remember correctly. I met that young boy yesterday. He’s seeking admission to Unilorin. We talked about life and his experiences too. After some back-and-forth conversations, he asked me where/if I could get him “SOCKS” because he knew I hack (write computer programs), so he assumed I would know. I didn’t get it initially; I assumed he was asking for the typical footwear socks, but when he mentioned VPN and tried to explain further, I began to understand.

Then we talked some more, and we spoke about his hacking attempts. His current hustle and the money he has made from “working”: Trust me, I have had a lot of vicarious (indirect) experiences with “working” and “working boys,” as the town I grew up in is arguably the epicenter of it, at least for a while, so I could relate to him, his motivations, and his ambitions. In the past couple of years, I’ve been an advocate of hacking money out as well; sometimes you just have to use what you’ve got to get what you need, and considering the realities in Nigeria, I considered it disingenuous to expect Nigerians, especially the younger ones that see their peers daily and have their struggles, to shy away from trying to eke their needs out.

I have tried to hack money out too; so to a great extent, I understand the yearnings and aspirations of this boy. Reflecting on his growth, it’s analogous to ours; we were all once young and naive. We had realistic expectations about what we could get out of life. We wanted to go to school, work hard, have lovely families, and contribute our quota to the advancement of our family, community, country, race, and humanity.

But we are in a dysfunctional country, and it has corrupted us all. We have realized that our modest expectations are illusory and most likely won’t happen. He realized this earlier because he could see his three other brothers, they all went to good schools, and what has come out of it as of yet? He can see it from his brothers’ peers like me, he has a sense of what works and what doesn’t. He must have concluded that for him to blow, he has to hack money out. How he came to this conclusion is irrelevant; what is important is that he has realized that in order to thrive in this society, you must hack success out. He can’t be convinced otherwise; he has seen the antecedents of our “successful” businessmen, politicians, teachers, athletes, writers, lawyers, etc.

This is the general attitude of most of us now in Nigeria, and what I think I have surmised, but I am not sure, is that this paradigm shift in mindset is leading us down a primrose path, and we will be stuck in a vicious cycle!

Now, what can you do about this? You can take a number of the available paths of least resistance—you can decide to leave this country, you can decide to hack money out with various methods, you can decide to stay individually upright and nothing more, or you can even decide to do nothing and many more—but by doing this, whether you realize it or not, you are inadvertently contributing to the cycle of hopelessness, and the abyss we are all currently digging will most likely swallow us all. Yielding to the path of least resistance is tempting and pragmatic, even natural—but it’s not prudent in our circumstances. Some of us must rise above our natural inclinations to correct this tragedy (trajectory)!

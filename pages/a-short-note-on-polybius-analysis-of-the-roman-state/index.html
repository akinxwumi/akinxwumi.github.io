A short note on Polybius analysis of the Roman state

I recently read book 6 of Polybius’s histories, and it is uncanny how a tract written such a long time ago is still very relevant for state building, or better yet, human coordination at a national scale, in our modern time, most especially in the African context.

Constitution and culture emanate from the need of men to coordinate, and the evolution from one form of them to another, as analyzed by Polybius, is an iterative attempt to arrive at the most optimal state. In his mind and in his days, the Roman constitution stands out. Not so long ago, I wrote that the meek shall not inherit Nigeria, and this realization begs several questions. How do we create a system that fosters cooperation and punishes, or better yet, stymies, those who attempt to cheat in the tragedy of the commons? How do we cultivate a sense of duty? How do we engender virtuous customs and laws? Et cetera.
